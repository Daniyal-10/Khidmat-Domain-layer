# Needs Assessment Unknown Unknowns

## 1. Hidden and Latent Needs
How do we model needs that exist but are completely unobservable or strongly denied by the beneficiary? Latent trauma, hidden debt, or concealed caste-based exclusion. If the evidence is invisible, how can an assessor confidently infer it without crossing into bias? The boundary between professional inference and algorithmic/assessor bias is exceptionally blurry.

## 2. The Decay Function of Need
We know needs expire, but *how* do they decay? If a family is given a 1-month food ration, does their food security need decay linearly over 30 days, or does it vanish instantly and reappear on day 31? If a system assumes continuous need, it risks over-delivery; if it assumes linear decay, it risks starvation. 

## 3. The Rights-Based Paradox (Entitlement vs. Vulnerability)
In government contexts (like India), some benefits are statutory entitlements (e.g., pensions for all widows regardless of income) while others are vulnerability-targeted. If Needs Assessment focuses strictly on "vulnerability," it might fail to properly capture "entitlement gaps" where a person is legally owed something but isn't suffering an acute crisis. Does Needs Assessment track "Rights Deprivation" as a Need?

## 4. Epistemological Drift
When an AI model reads 50 past assessment sessions and infers a new vulnerability score, is that an Assessment Session? Who is the assessor? If the AI is wrong, what is the dispute mechanism? As machine learning enters PMT (Proxy Means Testing), the ontology must be prepared to handle "Algorithmic Instrument" and "Algorithmic Assessor" with associated explainability tracking.
