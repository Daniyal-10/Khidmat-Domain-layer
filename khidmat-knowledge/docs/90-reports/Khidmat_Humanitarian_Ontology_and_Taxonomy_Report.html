Executive Summary

Modeling the domain before building the system.

Before any part of the Khidmat platform was implemented, work paused on a single question: what, precisely, does the organisation need to understand about a person, a household, a community, and a crisis, in order to respond well? That question cannot be answered by writing software. It has to be answered by modeling — naming the concepts a humanitarian caseworker already reasons with every day, defining how they relate to one another, and standardising the vocabulary used to describe them, so that "household," "case," "need," and "risk" mean exactly one thing across the entire organisation.

That modeling work is what this report reviews. It is not a report about software, folders, or engineering process — it is a review of the humanitarian knowledge itself: the ontology (the entities and relationships that represent humanitarian reality) and the taxonomy (the controlled vocabularies that classify it consistently). Thirteen humanitarian domains have now been modeled to this discipline, from the shape of a human life and a household's risk exposure, through intake, verification, and needs assessment, to case management, community context, programmes, donor and resource stewardship, and volunteer capacity.

An independent review of this knowledge model has since assessed it and found it substantially sound, with specific, identified work still remaining before it is considered complete — reviewed in full in §9 and §10 of this report. The purpose here is narrower and more fundamental: to show, domain by domain, how much of humanitarian reality has actually been captured.

Humanitarian Knowledge Model

A model of humanitarian reality, not of software.

Every concept below exists because a caseworker, verifier, or programme officer already reasons with it — the model gives it a stable definition, not a new meaning.

At its centre sits a shared understanding of a person — their stage of life, what they depend on, what they are capable of, their family relationships, and their health conditions — and of the household they belong to. Around that centre, a risk model describes how hazard, exposure, vulnerability, and resilience combine into a qualitative picture of a household's situation. From there, the model follows the humanitarian journey outward: a person is registered, their claims are verified, their needs are assessed, a case is opened and managed, support is delivered, and change over time is tracked — all set inside the community, programme, and resourcing context that makes a response possible.

Foundational Human & Risk Models

Shared Human Model

The person, independent of any single programme or case

Represents a human being the way humanitarian practice actually understands one: not as an age number, but as a position in a developmental journey. Nine lifecycle stages — infant, toddler, early childhood, school-age child, adolescent, young adult, adult, older adult, and elderly — each carry their own characteristic dependencies, capabilities, and vulnerabilities, so that a household's needs can be reasoned about differently depending on who is in it, not merely how many people are. Family structure is modeled richly enough to represent a household after bereavement — a deceased member remains a present structural fact, and a sole surviving earner who is also the family's caregiver is recognised as its own, especially fragile configuration. Capability, dependency, and health condition (including malnutrition staging and chronic and mental health conditions) are modeled as their own first-class vocabularies, not as side notes on a person record.

Lifecycle stage, capability, dependency type, family structure and kinship role, health condition
Relationships
Guardianship, kinship, support roles within a family unit
Used by

  • 13 dependency types
  • Guardian / legal guardian
  • SAM / MAM malnutrition staging
  • Widowhood as structural fact

Shared Risk Model

Why a household is vulnerable, and how vulnerability compounds

Models risk as a qualitative composition — hazard category, exposure, vulnerability, and household resilience combine into a characterised risk profile with a horizon (when harm might occur) and a trend (whether the picture is improving or deteriorating), deliberately kept free of numeric scoring so the reasoning stays legible rather than falsely precise. Resilience itself is modeled as seven distinct capacities — absorptive, adaptive, recovery, support redundancy, role substitution, buffering, and decision continuity — because a household's ability to withstand a shock is not one number, it is several different kinds of capacity that can each be present or absent independently. A compound-risk reasoning capability goes further: it recognises that an infant's vulnerability and an elderly person's frailty compound differently depending on who is providing care, and that two hazards striking at once — a seasonal flood during an economic crisis — is not simply the sum of two separate risks.

Distinctive strength

  • Exploitation & coercion indicators

The Casework Lifecycle

Registration

How a person's situation and needs first enter the system

Models the intake conversation as humanitarian practice actually runs it: a registrant (who may be the beneficiary themselves, a family proxy, a community member, or a volunteer) makes claims about a household's situation and needs, each claim carrying an epistemic basis — first-hand, second-hand, observational, or inferred — that determines how much weight it can bear. Claim quality is deliberately modeled along two independent axes: how complete the information is, and whether it is internally consistent — because a claim that is thorough but contradictory needs a different response than one that is consistent but incomplete. Situations are classified by trajectory (a sudden crisis reads differently from a long-standing structural condition) and by the specific event that triggered them — bereavement, job loss, injury, illness, displacement, domestic violence, disaster, or legal crisis — so that the same underlying need (food insecurity, say) is understood in the context that actually produced it.

Modeled
Registrant, beneficiary, household snapshot, situation, need, claim, evidence, support intervention, case, lead
Relationships
Situation contextualises need; claim is evidenced by; household observes the shared Person/Household
Reference implementation
The first domain modeled to the repository's canonical discipline, later used as the template for every other domain

Verification Operations

Turning a claim into a confirmed fact

Models what happens when a claim made at registration is checked against reality: a verification activity produces field observations and findings, each carrying a confidence level that can be composed from multiple observations rather than asserted as one number, contradictions between what was claimed and what was found are explicitly detected rather than silently overwritten, and specific conditions trigger re-verification when a case's circumstances may have changed. A human review step exists for findings that verification alone cannot resolve, and an escalation vocabulary distinguishes why a case needed human judgment.

Needs Assessment

Synthesizing evidence into an assessed, confidence-scored need

Sits between raw findings and an actionable need: an assessment session (conducted by a trained assessor, against a defined instrument and indicators) yields observations, which are synthesized into need assertions carrying an explicit confidence level and, where evidence conflicts, a conflict status rather than a silently resolved average. A calibration and consensus layer exists specifically so that different assessors' judgments can be checked against one another — an epistemic safeguard, not a data-entry convenience.

Case Management

Orchestrating the response to a verified, assessed need

Represents the case as the organising unit of the entire response: a case plan addresses specific needs through specific interventions, a referral connects the case to another organisation or service when Khidmat cannot meet a need directly, and a follow-up tracks whether a commitment was actually carried out. Case status, priority, origin, and closure reason are all modeled as controlled classifications rather than free text, so that every case can be reasoned about consistently regardless of who opened it or why.

Beneficiary Lifecycle

A person's relationship with the system over time, not a single snapshot

Models the fact that a beneficiary's engagement is not one case, but a history: an engagement stage (from first contact through active engagement to exit) and an event-sourced transition record allow the system to recognise that a household returning for a third time has not improved, or that a child enrolled in school at first registration is not enrolled at the second. A separate human-development-stage vocabulary is deliberately kept independent of engagement stage, so that a person's life stage is never confused with, or used as a proxy for, their administrative status with the organisation.

The Surrounding Context

Community Context

Represents the geographic, infrastructural, and social fabric a household sits inside — settlement type and formality, a full geographic hierarchy, environmental hazards distinct from the Risk Model's own hazard concepts, essential service access, seasonal events (a flood season, an agricultural cycle, an examination period), local organisations, and livelihood patterns. The most extensively classified domain in the repository by vocabulary breadth — twelve distinct controlled vocabularies covering the physical and social setting of a case.

Programmes

Models a structured programme as distinct from ad-hoc, case-by-case assistance: eligibility rules, intervention offerings with defined modality (cash, voucher, in-kind, service, or asset) and objective category, enrollment status and outcome, waitlist priority, and governance mechanisms — humanitarian overrides, appeals, and compliance checkpoints — for when a rigid eligibility rule needs a documented exception.

Support Delivery

Represents the moment support actually reaches a person: a delivery event, the custody chain from Khidmat to the person receiving it, proof of delivery, and an accountability record. A hard boundary is deliberately kept against the Donor & Resource domain — inventory ends and custody begins at a specific, modeled line, so a resource that has been allocated is not confused with one that has actually been handed over.

Impact

Provides the vocabulary for evaluation and measurement — indicator category, type, and hierarchy, attribution, and outcome structures. The thinnest domain in the model by content, appropriately, since it depends on longitudinal data that only accumulates once Beneficiary Lifecycle and the interventions it measures have been running in practice.

Volunteer Operations

Models the volunteer as a profile of skill, certification, availability, geographic coverage, language proficiency, and organisational affiliation, deliberately kept independent of any one organisation's specific volunteer programme. By design, it models only who a volunteer is and what they are qualified for — not the act of assigning them to a task, which belongs to the domains that actually do the assigning.

Donor & Resource

Models donor identity (a person or an organisation, never both at once), grants and contributions, and a resource model that distinguishes an abstract resource type from a tracked inventory item and a pre-delivery allocation decision. Includes a dedicated Islamic-giving vocabulary — zakat, sadaqah, sadaqah jariyah, waqf, fidya, kaffarah, and qurbani, with the eight zakat-eligible recipient categories (asnaf) — reflecting a real funding practice this organisation serves, modeled as its own controlled vocabulary rather than folded into a generic "donation type" label.

Taxonomy Coverage

A standardised humanitarian vocabulary.

Every classification below replaces what would otherwise be inconsistent free text — the same situation described one way by one caseworker and another way by the next.

Where the ontology says what exists, the taxonomy says which words are allowed to describe it, and what each one means. This is the layer that turns "the family is struggling" into a specific, comparable classification — which need category, which severity, which trigger event — so that two different caseworkers describing the same household arrive at the same understanding, and so that patterns can be seen across cases rather than trapped in individual narrative notes.

Registration

Standardises need categories (food, medical, shelter, education, livelihood, psychosocial) and their severities and durations; the circumstances that produce vulnerability (structural, crisis-triggered, progressive, or acute), the specific events that trigger them, and which areas of life a situation affects; and — its most distinctive contribution — a two-dimensional claim-quality vocabulary that separates how complete information is from whether it is internally consistent, so the two very different follow-up responses they require are never confused.

Verification Operations

Standardises verification status and confidence, the reasons a verification could not be completed, review decisions, and the conditions that trigger re-verification — replacing what would otherwise be an unstructured note about "why the volunteer couldn't confirm this" with a closed, comparable classification.

Needs Assessment

Standardises evidence type, confidence level, and conflict status; assessment modality, depth, and urgency; and a governance vocabulary for review, rejection, and consensus outcomes — making an assessor's judgment auditable rather than a private, undocumented call.

Community Context

The broadest single vocabulary set in the model: settlement type and formality, a full geographic hierarchy, environmental hazard exposure, essential service access, seasonal events, transportation and infrastructure types, livelihood patterns, and local organisation types — deliberately kept distinct from the Shared Risk Model's own hazard vocabulary and from the generic Shared organisation-type list, each with an explicit note on why the two do not overlap.

Programmes

Standardises intervention modality (cash, voucher, in-kind, service, asset), eligibility categories, enrollment status and outcome, termination reasons, waitlist priority, and thematic sector — the last of these serving, for now, as the working source for the sector vocabulary a future shared "humanitarian sector" concept is expected to formalise (see §10).

Support Delivery

Standardises delivery modality, the lifecycle a delivery passes through, the reasons a delivery attempt can fail or be exempted, and the proof-of-delivery and accountability vocabulary that keeps a custody chain auditable from allocation to the person who actually received it.

Volunteer Operations

Standardises volunteer status and type, skill category, certification type, availability and geographic coverage type, language proficiency, and training status — a complete qualification vocabulary, kept explicitly separate from the act of assigning a volunteer to a task, which is not this domain's concern.

Donor & Resource

The most extensively cross-checked vocabulary in the model: donor type and engagement pattern, anonymity level, funding lifecycle status, resource and stock-movement classification, storage type, allocation priority and status — plus the dedicated Islamic-giving vocabulary described in §4, deliberately exempted from regional synonym-collapsing because these are canonical religious-giving terms, not regional dialect variants of a generic concept.

A recurring pattern across this vocabulary work is worth naming directly: several domains discovered a near-duplicate concept already existing elsewhere and deliberately reconciled it rather than quietly re-defining it — Needs Assessment renamed one of its own classifications to avoid colliding with an identically-named one in Verification Operations; Beneficiary Lifecycle and Programmes each explicitly distinguish their own use of the word "graduated"; Community Context explains in its own governance notes exactly how its organisation vocabulary differs from the generic Shared one. This is what a genuinely single, consistent humanitarian language looks like in practice — not the absence of overlap risk, but overlap risk that gets caught and resolved.

Cross-Domain Knowledge Integration

How the domains reason together.

No domain owns its concepts in isolation — each one composes concepts owned elsewhere rather than redefining them, which is what makes a single, coherent model possible across thirteen separately developed domains.

A single person's path through the model illustrates the integration directly. The Human Model supplies who they are; Registration captures their situation using that same person definition; Verification checks the claims against reality, drawing on the Risk Model's vocabulary when a safety concern is present; Needs Assessment synthesizes the confirmed facts into an assessed need, aware of the Community Context the household sits in; Case Management opens a plan that may draw on a Programme's eligibility rules, a Donor & Resource allocation, or a Volunteer's qualification; Support Delivery executes it with a custody chain back to that same allocation; and Beneficiary Lifecycle and Impact together record whether the person's situation actually changed. No step re-defines a concept another domain already owns — each one references it.

Independent Review Summary

What an outside review of this knowledge model concluded.

An independent review of the full ontology and taxonomy has been completed, assessing it against its own internal consistency rules rather than taking its completeness on faith. Its conclusion, in knowledge terms rather than engineering ones:

Current maturity

The concepts and vocabulary across all thirteen domains are internally consistent — the same term means the same thing everywhere it is used, with no confirmed contradictions found across the model.

Major strengths

The controlled-vocabulary layer is the strongest asset reviewed — most domains actively identified and resolved near-duplicate concepts before they became genuine confusion, evidence of a mature, disciplined modeling practice rather than an accidental one.

Remaining knowledge work

Two foundational models (Human Model, Risk Model) still need restructuring into the same disciplined format the rest of the domains already follow; Case Management's decision reasoning has not yet been built; and the full model has not yet been exercised against one real, worked case end to end.

In short: the review found a knowledge model that represents its subject matter well, described with genuinely disciplined vocabulary, with clearly identified — not vague — work remaining before it can be considered finished. §10 sets that remaining work out directly.

Remaining Ontology & Taxonomy Work

What is left to finish, in knowledge terms.

Not software work — the modeling and vocabulary work still required before the knowledge layer can be considered complete.
Structural migration
The Human Model and Risk Model — already rich in content, described above — still need to be re-expressed in the same disciplined structural format every other domain now follows, so their concepts can be referenced and reasoned about with the same consistency.
Semantic refinement
A small number of concepts still need a cleaner formal link rather than a documented convention — most notably, the equivalence between the "case" concept as first opened at registration and the same case as later managed needs to be expressed as a direct semantic link, not only recorded as a governance decision.
Reasoning expansion
Case Management — the domain that decides what happens in response to a verified need — does not yet reason about that decision. Extending the kind of multi-factor, lifecycle-aware reasoning already demonstrated in the Risk Model into Case Management is the single most valuable next step in reasoning depth.
Vocabulary tidying
A handful of concepts in Registration's taxonomy are missing a written definition, and two residual cross-references still use an older citation style rather than the current one — small, already-identified corrections.
One worked scenario
The model has not yet been exercised end to end against a single, real humanitarian case — from intake through to a delivered response — to demonstrate that the concepts described across this report actually cohere in practice, not only in specification.
Knowledge layer freeze
Once the items above close, the knowledge model reaches a stable, frozen state — the point at which further changes to it become deliberate revisions rather than ongoing first-draft development.
Planned Knowledge Expansion

Future humanitarian domains — not overdue work.

Presented separately and deliberately: these are the next domains planned for the model, not gaps in what exists today.

Stage 9 — Programme Intelligence

A portfolio-level view above the single programme: how programmes depend on one another, how they are monitored, the results chains that connect activity to outcome, geographic coverage, and what makes a programme scalable and sustainable once it moves beyond a pilot.

Stage 10 — Community Intelligence

A deeper model of the community itself — resilience at the community rather than household level, community assets, local markets, schools and health facilities, local governance structures, conflict dynamics, and disaster exposure.

Stage 11 — Humanitarian Decision Intelligence

This remains ontology — a vocabulary for decisions, not a decision-making mechanism. It would model the professional concepts humanitarian decision-making already runs on: decision context, objective, constraint, trade-off, alternative, evidence, assumption, and uncertainty — giving future reasoning a vocabulary to reason in.

Stage 12 — Longitudinal Human Development

A long-horizon model of how a person's relationship to the organisation can change over years, illustrated below.

The model would run in one direction of intent — from receiving support in crisis, through stabilization and recovery, to self-reliance and resilience, and onward to a person eventually giving back to the same system that once supported them.

Collaboration & Next Steps

A foundation worth testing against real practice.

Thirteen humanitarian domains now share one consistent way of describing a person, a household, a risk, a case, and the context around it. That is a genuine and substantial foundation — but a model of humanitarian reality is only as good as its contact with humanitarian reality, and that contact is exactly what this review is seeking before the remaining and future work goes further.

Before extending into the planned domains described in §11, feedback from the people who actually do this work would be invaluable — not to validate what has been built, but to find what it has missed. In particular:

Are there humanitarian concepts, relationships, or considerations that experienced staff would expect to see represented, but do not yet appear anywhere in this model?

Are there operational workflows — ways cases, referrals, or interventions actually unfold in practice — that the model should represent more explicitly?

Are there classifications or terms already in everyday use — locally, regionally, or within specific programmes — that should be formally added to the taxonomy rather than left as informal shorthand?

Of the four planned future domains in §11, which would deliver the most value first, based on how the organisation actually expects to grow?

The goal of everything in this report is a model that reflects real humanitarian operations as faithfully as possible before implementation begins — and that is best achieved in conversation with the people closest to that operational reality, not in isolation from them.
